# Private Parking Fines: Know Your Rights

Private parking charges are **not the same** as council-issued PCNs. Understanding the difference is crucial to protecting your rights.

## Key Legal Differences

| | Council PCN | Private Parking Charge |

|---|---|---|

| **Legal basis** | Statute law | Contract law |

| **Issuer** | Local authority | Private company |

| **Appeal body** | Traffic Penalty Tribunal | POPLA / IAS |

| **Can it affect credit?** | No (but can use bailiffs) | Potentially via debt collectors |

## The Protection of Freedoms Act 2012

This landmark legislation changed the landscape of private parking enforcement:

- **Keeper liability** — parking companies can now pursue the registered keeper, not just the driver

- **But** they must follow strict procedures, including sending a Notice to Keeper within 14 days

- If they fail to follow procedures correctly, their claim against the keeper is **void**

## When Can You Challenge a Private Parking Fine?

You may have grounds to challenge if:

1. **No contract was formed** — signage was inadequate or absent

2. **The charge is disproportionate** — the Supreme Court ruled in *ParkingEye v Beavis* (2015) that charges must be proportionate

3. **The Notice to Keeper was late** — it must be received within 14 days of the alleged contravention

4. **You had a valid reason** — breakdown, medical emergency, etc.

5. **Incorrect vehicle details** — wrong registration, make, or model

## The POPLA Appeal Process

If the parking company is a member of the BPA (British Parking Association):

1. Appeal directly to the company first

2. If rejected, appeal to **POPLA** (Parking on Private Land Appeals)

3. POPLA's decision is binding on the company but not on you

4. If POPLA rules in your favour, the matter is closed

## Should You Just Ignore It?

**Generally, no.** While private parking companies previously had limited enforcement options, the situation has changed. Since 2012, they can pursue the registered keeper through the courts. Ignoring correspondence could lead to a County Court Judgment (CCJ).
