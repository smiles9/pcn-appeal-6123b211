# How to Appeal a Parking Ticket in the UK

Receiving a Penalty Charge Notice (PCN) can be stressful, but you have the legal right to challenge it. In fact, **many PCN appeals are successful** when the correct grounds are cited.

## Types of Parking Tickets in the UK

There are two main types of parking tickets:

### 1. Council-Issued PCNs
These are issued by local authorities or their contractors. They are governed by the **Traffic Management Act 2004** (in England and Wales) or the **Road Traffic Act 1991** (in Scotland).

### 2. Private Parking Charges
These are issued by private companies operating on private land. They are governed by **contract law** and the **Protection of Freedoms Act 2012**.

## Grounds for Appeal

Common grounds for a successful appeal include:

- **Inadequate signage** — signs were missing, obscured, or unclear
- **Faulty pay & display machines** — the machine was broken
- **Loading/unloading** — you were actively loading or unloading goods
- **Procedural errors** — the PCN was served incorrectly or out of time
- **Blue Badge holder** — you were displaying a valid disabled badge
- **Mitigating circumstances** — medical emergency, breakdown, etc.

## Deadlines You Must Know

| Stage | Deadline |
|-------|----------|
| Informal challenge (council PCN) | 14 days (to keep 50% discount) |
| Formal representation | 28 days from Notice to Owner |
| Appeal to tribunal (TPT/POPLA) | 28 days from rejection |

## Step-by-Step Appeal Process

1. **Gather evidence** — photos of signage, receipts, medical records
2. **Check the PCN for errors** — wrong date, location, or vehicle details
3. **Submit an informal challenge** within 14 days to preserve the discount
4. **If rejected, make a formal representation** to the council
5. **If still rejected, escalate** to the Traffic Penalty Tribunal (council) or POPLA (private)

## Tips for a Strong Appeal

- Be factual and concise in your letter
- Reference specific legislation
- Include photographic evidence
- Keep copies of all correspondence
- Never ignore a PCN — it won't go away
